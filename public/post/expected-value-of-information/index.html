Quantifying the Expected Value of Information

A Conceptual Introduction

Last updated on Aug 3, 2020 16 min read Bayesian Decision Analysis, Hugin, Uncertainty, VoI, R

TLDR

We sometimes have the option to purchase additional information (like paying for an experiment to be performed, or for a report from an expert consultant) to help us in problems of decision making under uncertainty. Deciding whether or not such services are worthwhile is challenging - how do we know how much valuable some information is before we get it? A powerful Bayesian procedure, which unfortunately goes by many names (such as preposterior decision analysis, multi-stage decision analysis, and Bayesian experimental design) allows us to quantify it’s expected value - in other words, it tells us how much we should be willing to pay for the information, before receiving it.

Decision Making under Uncertainty

Throughout industry, and even in our personal lives, we are required to make decisions without a perfect understanding of the problem. My PhD considered the problem of deciding whether or not risk mitigation measures were worth investing in, for structures in an uncertain state of damage. I quantified the expected value of inspection activities in this context, but I will discuss a less specific problem here.

Consider the case of a garage suggesting that you pay for an expensive repair on your car. They have let you know that there is a risk of a costly failure, so they are recommending replacing it. This problem can be visualed using an influence diagram. The below uses the RHugin library, an R API for the Hugin software, although my first impressions of the HydeNet library are that it could be suitable alternative.

library(RHugin)

prior_analysis <- RHugin::read.rhd('influence_diagram.net')
plot.RHuginDomain(x = decisionAnalysis, what = 'network')

I’ve drawn the above influence diagram in the Hugin GUI, and we can populate the tables using RHugin.

Elliptical nodes represent an uncertain outcome (the current condition of your car), rectangualr nodes represent decisions (whether or not to pay for the component to be replaced, as recommended by the garage) and diamond shaped nodes are the utilities (or costs) associated with each decision outcome. The arrows indicate dependency, for instance, the uncertain condition is dependent on whether or not the repair is performed (as is the amount you will pay for the repair). The expected cost of a breakdown then depends on the condition.

If we had a perfect understanding of the condition of our car, then we would know if it was going to breakdown and deciding whether this repair was worth doing would be easy. But we’re interested in solving more challenging problems, where it is sometimes a case of…

Decision making under uncertainty

We may be very unsure of the condition of the car. This implies that we consider there to be a significant chance that a repair is required (because we do not know enough to show that we don’t need the work done). Even so, we need some sort of quantiative approach to allow us to make coherent decisions. The uncertainty in the problem can be described using probability, and the consequences can also be quantified. Let’s assume the below values for now (though there is nothing special about them):

A Note on Utility Theory

The optimal solutions in a decision analysis problem are those that correspond to a maximum expected pay-off (or a minimum expected cost). This mathematically coherent approach was first proposed by John von Neumann and Oskar Morgenstern. However, it may not always be appropriate to map financial consequences of decision outcomes to a linear scale.

Consider a possible cost of $30,000.

Is this really twice as bad as an equally likely cost of $15,000? It depends.

The higher cost, could have additional knock-on effects. It may require a loan, or some other expenditure to be deferred. If we are completing a decision analysis on behalf of a large company, then it could be reasonable to compare costs of this magnitude on a simple linear scale. However, if the decision maker was a small start-up, this assumption may not be helpful.

As a result, we may need to transform monetary costs (or pay-offs) onto a utility scale. The optimal decision is then associated with the expected maximum utility. There is significant scientific literature on utuility functions, but a concise discussion in the context of Bayesian decision theory is provided in the book from John Pratt, Howard Raiffa and Robert Schlaiffer. Some default functions are available, but there is also the option of fitting a function from some points, where money vs. utility has been defined by the decision maker.

So the expected value of some experiment is based on what we think it might find - that doesn’t sound reliable…

…hold up a minute. We are using this method to tackle problems of decision making under uncertainty. What we are doing is propagating a model of uncertainty through our analysis, so that we can account for it in a coherent way. As much as we would prefer to know things precisely, this won’t always be the case and failing to account for uncertainty can result in bad decision making.

Now, if we see the resut of a VoI analysis and it doesn’t feel right, this could mean a few things: - the problem is not intuitive, and our intinctive reaction is overlooking some complexity. If we suspect this is the case, then maybe it is better to trust the analysis, as it can easily be documented, critiqued and re-visited. - we’ve missed out some prior information. This can easily be fixed as Bayesian methods are happy to accomodate prior models. In my post on Bayesian logistic regression I showed how prior predictive simulation can help identify sensible priors, even in more complicated Bayesian models.
